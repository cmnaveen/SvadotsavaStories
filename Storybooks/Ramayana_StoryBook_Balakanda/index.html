The Ramayana

Bala Kanda

A magical storybook journey through the first book of the Ramayana — told for young readers in 42 beautifully illustrated pages.

Page 1 of 42

The Question of the Perfect Hero

Long ago, a gentle sage named Valmiki sat beside a quiet river, turning one question over and over in his mind. In all the wide world, he wondered, who is the bravest, the kindest, and the most truthful person alive? Just then the wandering singer Narada drifted down from the sky, smiled, and said, "Let me tell you about Rama, the noble prince of Ayodhya." As Valmiki listened, his heart grew so full that he longed to know more.

Page 2 of 42

The Sorrowful Bird and the First Poem

The very next morning, as he walked beside the water, Valmiki saw a cruel hunter strike down one of a pair of singing birds, and the other bird wept with grief. Such deep sorrow welled up in the sage that it flowed from his lips as a verse, the very first poem the world had ever heard. Then, in a shower of golden light, Lord Brahma himself appeared and said, "Sing the whole story of Rama in just this way." And so the great tale began.

Page 3 of 42

The Golden City of Ayodhya

Where did Rama's story begin? It began in a shining city called Ayodhya, golden and grand, ruled by the wise old King Dasaratha. The streets rang with music and laughter, and no one ever went hungry. Yet behind the tall palace walls, the king carried a quiet sorrow that no one could see.

Page 4 of 42

The King's Quiet Wish

King Dasaratha had everything a king could wish for, except a child to call his own. "If only my palace echoed with little footsteps," he would often sigh. So his trusted minister Sumantra came to him with an idea. They would invite the holy young sage Rishyasringa to perform a sacred fire-ceremony, and perhaps the heavens would answer at last.

Page 5 of 42

The Wise Sage Rishyasringa

And so, with drums and lamps and showers of flower petals, the gentle sage Rishyasringa was welcomed into Ayodhya, and he brought hope with him like sunshine after rain. On the banks of the river Sarayu, he helped the king begin a great and holy fire-sacrifice.

Page 6 of 42

The Great Fire Sacrifice

The priests chanted, sweet smoke curled up toward the clouds, and the sacred fire blazed with everyone's hopeful prayers. But high above the sky, the gods were watching, and they were deeply worried. They believed this might be the perfect moment to ask for help against a terrible demon-king named Ravana.

Page 7 of 42

A Plea in the Heavens

And who was this Ravana? He was a cruel demon with ten heads and twenty glaring eyes, so strong and so proud that he frightened gods and people alike. Trembling, the gods flew together to Lord Vishnu, folded their hands, and begged him, "Only you can save us. Please help us."

Page 8 of 42

Vishnu's Great Promise

Lord Vishnu smiled his calm and knowing smile. "Do not be afraid," he said. "I myself will be born on Earth as the sons of King Dasaratha, and I will set the world right again." And at that very moment, far below on Earth, something wondrous began to rise out of the sacrificial flames.

Page 9 of 42

The Gift from the Sacred Fire

Out of the fire stepped a tall, glowing being, holding a golden bowl of sweet, magical pudding. "Share this with your queens," he said, and then he vanished. The king's hands trembled with joy as he gave it to them, for very soon the whole palace would have something wonderful to celebrate.

Page 10 of 42

Four Princes Are Born

Before long, the palace rang with the happy cries of not one but four baby princes: Rama, Bharata, Lakshmana, and little Shatrughna. And far away in the deep forests, the gods were quietly busy too, creating mighty monkey-warriors, secret friends that Rama would one day need on a very great adventure.

Page 11 of 42

Brave Princes and Hidden Helpers

The four little princes grew up clever, strong, and kind, and best of all, they loved one another dearly. But Rama shone the brightest of them all, with a heart so gentle that everyone adored him. And just as he was nearing sixteen, a powerful visitor came striding into the palace with an urgent plea.

Page 12 of 42

The Sage's Important Request

Into the throne room strode the great sage Vishwamitra, his eyes flashing. "Demons are spoiling our holy prayers in the forest," he declared. "Send young Rama to protect us." The king's heart sank, for he did not wish to send his precious son into such danger. But the wise teacher Vasishtha gently leaned in and said, "You must trust the sage."

Page 13 of 42

A Father's Worry, A Wise Word

So, blinking back his tears, the king allowed Rama and his faithful brother Lakshmana to set off into the wild with Vishwamitra. They were two brave boys with bows on their backs, stepping out into the unknown. As they walked along the river Sarayu, the sage thought to himself, "First, I must make these young princes ready."

Page 14 of 42

Secret Chants by the River

He taught them two secret chants called Bala and Atibala, magical words that kept them strong, never tired, and never afraid. With these chants humming softly in their hearts, the princes pressed on, deeper and deeper, until they came to a forest so dark and silent that not a single bird dared to sing.

Page 15 of 42

The Dark Forest of Tataka

This gloomy wood belonged to a fierce demoness named Tataka, and everyone for miles around was afraid of her. "Be brave, Rama," said the sage softly. Rama lifted his bow, took one steady breath, and stepped forward, for he had made up his mind to bring peace back to this frightened land.

Page 16 of 42

Rama Brings Back the Light

Out rushed Tataka with a terrible roar, but Rama stood as calm as a still pond, and with a single swift arrow he set the whole forest free. At once the sunlight came pouring through and the birds returned, singing once more. The delighted sage decided that this brave prince deserved a wondrous reward.

Page 17 of 42

Gifts of the Shining Weapons

And what a reward it was. Vishwamitra gave Rama glittering celestial weapons that appeared like friendly, shining spirits, all ready to obey him. "Use these only for what is good," the sage said, and Rama promised with all his heart. Then onward they went, to guard the sage's sacred fire-sacrifice.

Page 18 of 42

Guarding the Holy Prayers

For six long days and nights, the brothers kept careful watch over the chanting sages. Then, all at once, the sky turned black, and down swooped two roaring demons, Maricha and Subahu, hurling rocks and ash to spoil the holy prayers. But Rama was ready, with his bow already in his hand.

Page 19 of 42

The Sacrifice Is Saved

Quick as a flash of lightning, Rama loosed a mighty arrow that sent Maricha tumbling head over heels clear across the ocean, and he scattered the other demons too. The sacrifice was saved, and the grateful sages cheered. "Come now," said Vishwamitra warmly, "let us journey on to Mithila." And along the way, he had the most wonderful stories to share.

Page 20 of 42

A Journey and a Wonderful Tale

As they walked along, Vishwamitra pointed up at the great snowy mountains and began the tale of the holy river Ganga. "Long ago," he said, "a proud king named Sagara held a grand horse-ceremony, but it ended in the strangest mystery you ever heard." And Rama and Lakshmana leaned in close, eager for every word.

Page 21 of 42

King Sagara and the Sacred Horse

"The sacred horse simply vanished into thin air," said the sage. "So King Sagara sent his sixty thousand sons to search for it in every corner of the world. They dug deep, far down into the earth itself, and there they made a terrible mistake."

Page 22 of 42

A Mistake and a Lesson

"They found the horse beside a wise sage named Kapila, who was deep in quiet meditation. But instead of bowing politely, they shouted at him so rudely that in a single flash they were burned to ashes," said Vishwamitra, shaking his head. "And many years passed before a noble prince called Bhagiratha set out to free their lost souls."

Page 23 of 42

Bhagiratha's Mighty Effort

"Now Bhagiratha prayed and prayed with all his heart," Vishwamitra went on, "until at last the gods agreed to send the holy river Ganga rushing down from heaven to earth. But she came down so wild and so mighty that she might have shattered the whole world, and only Lord Shiva was strong enough to catch her."

Page 24 of 42

The River Falls from Heaven

"So Shiva caught the roaring Ganga in his tangled hair and let her flow out soft and gentle, to bless the earth and free those ancestors at last," said the sage. "When we work together in this way, even the impossible becomes possible. Once, the gods and demons even churned the whole great ocean side by side."

Page 25 of 42

The Churning of the Ocean

"And by churning that ocean together, they drew out the very nectar of life. That is what working together can do," Vishwamitra finished with a smile. Just then, as they neared Mithila, they came upon a hermitage that stood silent and overgrown, as though no one had lived there for years and years. Rama could not help wondering what its story might be.

Page 26 of 42

Ahalya's Long Wait

"Long ago," said Vishwamitra gently, "this was the home of the great sage Gautama and his wife, Ahalya. But Ahalya was once led into a grave mistake, and in his anger Gautama declared that she must live here all alone, unseen by every passing eye, fasting and praying, until one pure and noble soul should come to set her free." He turned to Rama with knowing eyes. "That soul, my boy, is you. Step inside, and let her welcome you."

Page 27 of 42

Ahalya Is Freed

As Rama and Lakshmana stepped softly into the quiet hermitage, there stood Ahalya, glowing after her long years of prayer. The two princes bowed low and touched her feet with deep respect, and at that very moment her long penance came to an end. She welcomed them with loving care, the heavens rained down flowers, and the sage Gautama returned at last to her side. Then the travelers marched onward, until the festive gates of Mithila came into view.

Page 28 of 42

Welcome to Mithila

The wise King Janaka welcomed them warmly, though he could not help wondering who these two shining young princes might be. When Vishwamitra told him their names, Janaka's whole face lit up, and he began to tell them about his beloved daughter, Sita, and a mighty test of strength.

Page 29 of 42

The Earth-Child and the Mighty Bow

"My Sita was no ordinary child, for she rose up out of the earth itself as I plowed my field," said Janaka. "And she will marry only the one who is strong enough to lift and bend the giant bow of Lord Shiva." Rama looked up at the colossal bow, smiled softly to himself, and stepped forward to try.

Page 30 of 42

Rama Lifts the Unliftable Bow

As calm as could be, Rama lifted that enormous bow as easily as if it were a single flower. And as he bent it to string it, the bow snapped with a boom like thunder that shook the whole hall. The crowd leapt up cheering, and Sita stepped forward, her heart singing with joy.

Page 31 of 42

Sita Is Won

When the great bow lay broken, King Janaka's heart overflowed with joy. "By his strength and his valour," he declared, "the noble Rama has won my daughter Sita as his bride!" All of Mithila erupted in cheers, and Janaka sent his swiftest messengers galloping off to Ayodhya, to carry the happy news to King Dasaratha.

Page 32 of 42

The King Journeys to Mithila

Overjoyed, King Dasaratha set out for Mithila with a grand royal parade of elephants, drums, and fluttering banners. And when the two kind kings finally met, they clasped hands like old friends and decided to make this happy day grander still.

Page 33 of 42

Four Brothers, Four Brides

"Why stop at just one wedding?" said King Janaka with a twinkle in his eye. "Let all four of your sons marry the four young princesses of my family." Everyone agreed with delight, and at once the palace was decorated for the grandest wedding the world had ever seen.

Page 34 of 42

The Grand Wedding

Mithila glittered like a dream, with a thousand tiny lamps and flower garlands everywhere one looked. The four happy couples gathered together as music swirled all around them. And smiling shyly, the brides and grooms made ready to take their sacred vows around the holy fire.

Page 35 of 42

Sacred Vows by the Fire

Hand in hand, they walked slowly around the fire, promising to love and care for one another always. When the ceremonies were done, the joyful families bade Mithila goodbye and set off for home. But they had barely begun their journey when the sky turned dark and a sudden storm blocked their path.

Page 36 of 42

A Storm on the Road Home

Out of the gloom strode a fierce warrior-sage named Parasurama, his great axe gleaming on his shoulder, furious that Shiva's mighty bow had been broken. The whole royal family froze, their hearts pounding. But Rama simply stepped forward, calm and respectful, and bowed.

Page 37 of 42

The Warrior's Challenge

"If you are truly as strong as they say," thundered Parasurama, "then string this mighty bow of Lord Vishnu." Rama only smiled his gentle smile, accepted the challenge without a single boastful word, and lifted the heavy bow into his hands.

Page 38 of 42

Calm Strength Wins

With the greatest ease, Rama strung the giant bow, and all at once a warm golden light shone around him, showing that he was no ordinary prince, but the Lord himself. At that wondrous sight, all of Parasurama's anger and pride melted away into deep and humble awe.

Page 39 of 42

Peace at Last

Parasurama bowed low to Rama and quietly walked away toward the faraway mountains, to pray there in peace. The storm clouds scattered, the blue sky shone down once more, and the happy travelers continued on their way home.

Page 40 of 42

The Joyful Homecoming

And what a homecoming it was! Ayodhya greeted them with cheering crowds, flower-strewn streets, and a glowing lamp in every window. The princes and their lovely brides entered the palace, where their mothers stood waiting with open arms and happy tears.

Page 41 of 42

A Warm Welcome for the Brides

The three queen-mothers welcomed the new brides with sweet treats, glowing lamps, and loving blessings upon their foreheads. At long last the family was whole and happy. And so Rama and Sita began a beautiful new life together, side by side.

Page 42 of 42

A Happy Beginning

And so Rama and Sita lived joyfully together, loved by everyone for their kindness and their truth. Here our first book gently comes to a close. But all of this was only the very beginning of their great and timeless adventure.
