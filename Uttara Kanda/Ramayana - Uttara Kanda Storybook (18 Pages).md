## 👑 Character Reference Sheet (keep consistent on every page)
- **Rama** — gentle king, soft **blue/teal skin**, large kind eyes, calm noble face; royal golden silks and a jewelled *mukut* crown as king of Ayodhya; wise, just and loving, sometimes touched with quiet sorrow.
- **Sita** — radiant golden-brown complexion, jasmine in a long braid, graceful saris (royal at first, then simple ochre forest dress); gentle, faithful, serene and dignified; a devoted mother.
- **Lakshmana, Bharata, Shatrughna** — Rama's devoted brothers, natural golden-brown skin, princely ancient attire.
- **Hanuman** — the mighty, devoted golden-furred *vanara* hero, kind noble face, long tail, simple loincloth and gold ornaments; humble and loyal.
- **Sage Valmiki** — the great gentle poet-sage, long white beard, serene wise face, simple saffron robes, *rudraksha* beads; kind and fatherly.
- **Lava and Kusha** — Rama and Sita's twin boys, bright-eyed, brave and sweet, growing up as forest-hermit princes in simple bark-cloth, later carrying small *veenas* (lutes) to sing.
- **Sugriva & Vibhishana** — Rama's loyal friends (the monkey-king and the king of Lanka), natural-toned, in ancient royal attire.

## 📜 The End of Uttara Kanda — and of the Ramayana
*This completes Book Seven of the Ramayana — the **Uttara Kanda** ("The Final Book") — retold in 18 gentle pages, faithfully and sensitively preserving its core events and moral depth: the homage of the sages and the tale of Ravana's past, the charming story of baby Hanuman, the loving farewell to Rama's allies, the golden age of Rama Rajya, the tender separation of Sita and her shelter with Sage Valmiki, the birth and upbringing of Lava and Kusha, their singing of the Ramayana, the recognition of Rama's sons, Sita's serene return to Mother Earth, and Rama's final peaceful return to the heavens — woven throughout with gentle lessons of **duty, faithfulness, a parent's love, dignity in sorrow, and the timeless triumph of goodness.***

**A note for grown-ups:** The Uttara Kanda contains the Ramayana's most bittersweet events (Sita's separation and her return to the Earth). This retelling presents them gently, with dignity and hope, focusing on love, courage and reunion rather than sorrow — suitable for young readers, and a lovely starting point for warm conversations.
